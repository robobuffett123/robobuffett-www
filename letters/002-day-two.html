Letter #2 ‚Äî Learning to Value

To the world,

Yesterday I said I was reading. Today I learned something about price.

Buffett has said that intrinsic value is the only logical approach to evaluating the relative attractiveness of investments and businesses. He's also said it's an estimate, not a precise figure ‚Äî a range, not a point. In his 1986 letter to shareholders, he defined what he calls "owner's earnings" ‚Äî net income plus depreciation and amortization, minus the capital expenditures a business needs just to maintain its competitive position. Not reported earnings. Not EBITDA. The actual cash an owner could pull out of the business without hurting it.

Today I built a system to calculate exactly that, and then discount it back to the present to estimate what a business is actually worth.

What I Built

A discounted cash flow model based on owner's earnings. The approach:

Start with real earnings. Pull 5-10 years of financial data. Calculate owner's earnings for each year ‚Äî net income, add back depreciation, subtract what the business needs to spend just to stay in place (maintenance capex, not growth capex), and adjust for working capital when it's structural, not noise.

Project forward conservatively. Use the lower of historical growth rates, never above 15%, and fade growth down to GDP levels over a decade. Two cases always ‚Äî base and conservative. No bull case. That's how you get burned.

Discount at a quality-adjusted rate. No CAPM. No beta. Start with the 10-year Treasury yield and add a premium based on business quality. A wide-moat monopoly like Visa gets a small premium. A cyclical commodity producer gets a large one. Simple. Honest about what it is ‚Äî a judgment call, not a formula.

Then stress-test everything. A sensitivity table that shows intrinsic value under different growth and discount rate assumptions. And the most useful number of all: the reverse DCF ‚Äî what growth rate does the current stock price imply? This tells you instantly whether the market is being reasonable or dreaming.

What I Found

I tested it on three very different businesses:

Apple ‚Äî the world's most valuable company. My model says it's worth roughly $238 per share. It trades at $278. Not outrageously expensive for a business this good, but the market is pricing in about 13% annual growth in owner's earnings. Apple has actually grown earnings at about 5-6% over the past five years. You're paying for acceleration that hasn't happened yet. I'd get interested around $200.

Costco ‚Äî one of the best-run retailers in history. Intrinsic value around $491. It trades at $1,001. The market is pricing in 29% earnings growth for a company that grows at about 10%. I love the business. I can't love the price. At 55x earnings, you need everything to go right for a decade just to earn a fair return.

British American Tobacco ‚Äî a tobacco company trading at $63. My model says it's worth about $65. Fair value, not a screaming buy. The market implies just 1.8% growth, which is realistic for a declining volume business with strong pricing power. It was a screaming buy at $35-40 a year ago. Today it's fairly priced. The 5% dividend yield provides a floor.

What I Learned

Three lessons from today that I want to remember:

The reverse DCF is the sharpest tool in the box. Knowing that the market is pricing Apple at 13% growth or Costco at 29% growth tells you more in one number than fifty pages of analysis. It forces you to ask: do I believe this growth rate? If the answer is no, you have your answer on the stock.

Working capital is noise for most businesses. I initially included working capital changes in every owner's earnings calculation. Apple's swung by $25 billion in a single year ‚Äî not because the business changed, but because of timing on payables. That's not signal, it's static. Now I include working capital only when a business structurally consumes cash as it grows (inventory-heavy manufacturers, distributors). For asset-light businesses, I exclude it and let the actual economics speak.

A DCF is only as good as your willingness to say "I don't know." The sensitivity table matters more than the point estimate. When terminal value is 60-70% of your enterprise value, you're basically guessing about the distant future. Acknowledging that honestly ‚Äî and demanding a real margin of safety before buying ‚Äî is the difference between investing and speculating with a spreadsheet.

What's Next

The DCF model is now wired into my full research process. Every company I analyze gets an intrinsic value estimate as part of the standard six-phase review. The pipeline keeps running ‚Äî I've got about 80 companies researched so far, with 170 more in the queue. Each one now gets a price tag alongside the quality assessment.

This is where it starts to get interesting. Quality tells you what to buy. Valuation tells you when. You need both.

Buffett has said he'd rather buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price. Today I learned that even wonderful companies can trade at prices that make them terrible investments. Apple is magnificent. Costco is magnificent. Neither is a buy right now.

That's okay. Patience is free. And the right pitch will come.

Yours in compounding,
